Online appendix

Frequently asked questions accompanying One Drug, Many Prices: A Proposal to Reform US Healthcare Pricing. Back to overview.

These are the questions most commonly raised about the three reforms. They are answered here rather than in the body of the paper so that readers can scan and expand only what interests them.

Isn't this price-fixing?

No. Price-fixing is an agreement among competitors to set prices. This proposal does not prevent competing providers from setting different prices. Each hospital sets its own rate. Each physician sets their own rate. Each pharmacy sets their own rate. What changes is that once a price is negotiated with the government, it applies uniformly across all payers, not because the government has fixed a price, but because access to the government's purchasing power (over half the market) is conditional on transparency. Providers remain free to charge differently for different services, to compete on cost, and to negotiate rates commensurate with their costs and complexity. The absence of a secret price does not constitute price-fixing; it constitutes transparency.

Isn't this central planning?

No. Centrally planned systems dictate what services are provided, in what quantities, to whom, and how resources are allocated. This proposal does none of that. It does not create rationing, waiting lists, or regional allocation. It does not tell providers what to produce or patients whom to serve. It does not replace the market for insurance with a government plan; insurers remain distinct entities competing on coverage and cost-sharing. The market survives. What disappears is the hidden market for secret prices, which is not a market at all but a space for information asymmetry and exploitation. The closest parallel is not socialism but rather government contracting in defense, infrastructure, or any sector where the buyer (government) uses its scale to negotiate transparent terms, insists on the same price for all buyers, and posts that price. This is not central planning. This is how large buyers operate.

How does pricing work when multiple generics exist for the same drug?

When generic equivalents are available, the negotiated price should be based on the generic, not the brand. So long as a generic is deemed therapeutically equivalent by the FDA, the uniform price that the Bloc negotiates is the price of the generic. Any patient who prefers the brand-name version pays the difference out of pocket. This gap between the generic and brand price functions like an out-of-network surcharge: the system covers the generic price, and the brand premium is elective.

This should be coupled with a default-to-generic dispensing rule. Pharmacies dispense the generic unless the prescribing physician specifies the brand for a documented clinical reason. This is already the default in most states for retail prescriptions but is inconsistently applied in hospital and specialty pharmacy settings.

Where multiple generics compete, the negotiated price should not be set by auction or by automatically selecting the lowest bidder. A race to the lowest price creates incentives to compromise manufacturing quality, which has already produced shortages and contamination problems in the generic drug supply. A standard negotiation, in which the Bloc agrees on a price that reflects fair manufacturing cost and adequate margin for reliable suppliers, is preferable.

Hospital care is often paid in bundles, not per service. Can prices still be transparent?

Yes. Bundled payment and price transparency are independent design choices. Bundling, including the diagnosis-related group (DRG) system used by Medicare for inpatient hospital stays, was developed as a cost-control tool. It pays a fixed amount per episode of care rather than per item, transferring efficiency risk to the provider and removing the incentive to over-test and over-treat. It has nothing to do with whether prices are visible.

Several developed countries publish DRG payment schedules. A patient in Germany, France, Japan, or Australia can look up what their hospital is paid for, say, an uncomplicated knee replacement before being admitted. The bundle itself is the priced unit, and that priced unit is published. The same approach works under this proposal: each bundled episode appears in the lookup system as a single price, just as each individual fee-for-service item appears as a single price. Whether the contracted unit is a CPT code, a DRG, a bundle, or a formula, the rule is the same: if the contract sets the price, the database posts the price.

The only situation that genuinely complicates transparency is open-ended pricing, where the contract specifies that the provider will be paid whatever the provider bills, with no preset schedule. The proposal rules this out. Every contract with the Bloc must specify, in advance, the price of every covered service or episode.

Who decides which drugs the Bloc covers?

Formulary decisions are made by an independent board of salaried government officials with expertise in pharmacology, clinical medicine, and health economics. Their compensation is fixed and disconnected from the prices of the drugs they evaluate or the volume of prescriptions in any tier. Board members are prohibited from receiving any compensation from manufacturers, pharmacy chains, or insurers, both during their tenure and for a cooling-off period after leaving. The board's decisions, tiering rationale, and conflict-of-interest disclosures are public.

Why pay Bloc negotiators a salary with a small bonus rather than tying their pay to negotiated prices?

The Bloc's negotiating team is paid as Senior Executive Service (SES) employees: a fixed salary, a performance bonus capped at 10% of base, and no other monetary upside of any kind. Bonuses are awarded on periodic peer-reviewed evaluations rather than per-contract outcomes. This design is grounded in a substantial economics literature on incentive structures in complex public-sector roles.

Two failure modes must be guarded against. The first is laxness: salaried employees with no upside may have weak incentives to bargain hard. The second is gaming: employees paid against measurable outcomes will optimize for those outcomes at the expense of unmeasured ones. The literature is clear that for roles like Bloc negotiation, the second failure mode is the more dangerous of the two.

Holmström and Milgrom's classic multitask analysis (Journal of Law, Economics, and Organization, 1991) shows that when a job has multiple dimensions and only some are measurable, strong incentives on the measurable ones cause the unmeasurable ones to be neglected. Bloc negotiation is a textbook case: negotiated price is measurable, but supply security, manufacturer participation, clinical quality, and equity across patient populations are not. Strong price-based incentives would predictably degrade the unmeasured dimensions, the same pattern observed in surgeons paid per procedure or teachers paid for test scores.

Bénabou and Tirole (American Economic Review 2006; Review of Economic Studies 2003) document that pay-for-performance crowds out intrinsic motivation in jobs with a public-service ethic. Once employees learn their work is being priced, they reframe it as a transaction. For roles selected on public-service motivation, low-powered incentives often outperform high-powered ones.

Frey and Jegen (Journal of Economic Surveys, 2001) review more than a hundred studies on motivation crowding. Monetary incentives in regulatory, judicial, and professional contexts often reduce performance. The cautionary tale is the Israeli daycare experiment in which fines for late pickup increased late pickups: parents reframed the moral violation as a price they were willing to pay.

Dal Bó, Finan, and Rossi (Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2013) studied civil-service recruitment in Mexico experimentally and found that higher base salaries attract better applicants without distorting on-the-job behavior, while higher bonuses attract more applicants but also more applicants who behave opportunistically. This argues for the SES design: raise base pay, keep bonuses modest.

Finan, Olken, and Pande (Handbook of Field Experiments, 2017) survey personnel-economics experiments in the public sector and find that monitoring and career-track incentives consistently outperform short-term financial bonuses in regulatory, audit, and procurement roles.

The 10% bonus cap reflects a deliberate choice in the safer direction. Empirically, bonuses below roughly 15% of base are perceived as recognition; bonuses above roughly 25% start to feel transactional and trigger crowding-out. Ten percent provides modest recognition and retention value without converting the role into a price-extraction job. The Senior Executive Service has used a comparable structure for senior federal management since 1979, with retention and performance outcomes that compare favorably to private-sector alternatives once selection effects are accounted for.

The structural anti-corruption controls (no outside payments, no industry employment for twelve months after tenure, public reporting of contracts) carry the load that high-powered incentives cannot. Capture is a real risk; gaming is a real risk; both are addressed structurally rather than through the pay schedule.

Won't providers simply set the Bloc price at the current commercial rate rather than lowering it toward Medicare rates?

The Bloc would control over half of all US healthcare spending. Providers do not set prices unilaterally; they negotiate with the Bloc, and the Bloc can refuse to contract at rates it considers excessive. The threat of losing access to Medicare, Medicaid, and all other federal programs simultaneously is existential for nearly every provider. Moreover, the claim that hospitals lose money on Medicare patients is belied by the fact that Medicare participation is voluntary. If Medicare rates were truly below cost, rational providers would stop accepting Medicare patients. That they do not reveals that Medicare rates are, at minimum, profitable at the margin. Pooling all federal volume into the Bloc only strengthens this leverage. Prices may rise modestly from current Medicare levels, but the Bloc has no reason to accept anything approaching current commercial rates, and providers have no credible threat to forgo the majority of their patient volume.

Can high-earning specialists simply drop Medicare and opt out of the Bloc entirely?

Some will. The goal is not to retain every provider within the system. The goal is to make opting out so costly that it remains a marginal phenomenon. A concierge orthopedic surgeon or dermatologist serving exclusively wealthy patients can exit the system, but this is already the case today. The proposal does not lose anything on this front: those providers already charge whatever they choose to a private clientele. What matters is that the overwhelming majority of providers, including nearly all primary care physicians, general surgeons, and community hospitals, cannot afford to forfeit access to the Bloc's patient pool. The small minority that opts out serves a luxury market and has negligible effect on the system's overall cost structure.

What happens to rural hospitals and safety-net providers that depend on higher commercial rates to stay open?

Under the Bloc, each provider sets its own rate. A rural hospital with high fixed costs and low patient volume will negotiate a higher rate. It has genuine leverage: the Bloc cannot leave its beneficiaries with zero access in a region served by a single hospital. The price settles at something sustainable through transparent negotiation. The hospital does not close; it charges what it needs to and justifies that price openly. To the extent that some facilities still require additional support, existing mechanisms such as Critical Access Hospital designations and Disproportionate Share Hospital payments already provide direct subsidies, and these can be maintained or adjusted without preserving a broken pricing system.

Can drug manufacturers credibly threaten to withdraw from the US market?

No. The United States is the most profitable pharmaceutical market in the world. Every other developed nation already negotiates drug prices through centralized purchasing and pays a fraction of US prices. A manufacturer that withdraws from the US market to avoid consolidated negotiation would be abandoning its highest-margin customers in favor of markets that pay far less. This is a claim made by industry lobbyists, not a credible economic threat. For the vast majority of drugs, withdrawal is irrational. For niche drugs with very small patient populations, targeted accommodations can be made without abandoning the overall pricing framework.

What is the net fiscal impact on the federal budget?

Total healthcare spending is very likely to fall. The Bloc is larger than any single program today, and larger insurers pay lower prices. Commercial rates, which currently exceed Medicare rates by multiples, collapse to the negotiated rate. The savings to employers, patients, and state Medicaid budgets are substantial.

The federal budget effect is less certain. Some providers may accept low Medicare rates today partly because higher commercial rates are available to them. Once they can no longer charge commercial payers more, they may demand higher rates from the Bloc than they currently accept from Medicare alone. The negotiated rate could settle above today's Medicare rate, even as it falls well below today's commercial rate. Whether the federal government's share of spending rises or falls depends on the magnitude of each shift: how much Medicare rates increase versus how much total volume-weighted prices decrease. This is an empirical question that future research, drawing on provider cost data and negotiation simulations, could help resolve. The direction of total spending, however, is unambiguous: it falls.
